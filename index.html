Σ Formula Reference
ⓘ All formulas use natural logarithm (ln). CI derivation uses the delta method on the appropriate scale. Approximations are flagged with ⚠.

OR ⇄ RR (Relative Risk)

OR → RR (requires baseline risk p₀)
RR = OR / (1 − p₀ + p₀ × OR)
Exact conversion. p₀ = control group event proportion. Valid for all risks.
RR → OR
OR = RR × (1 − p₀) / (1 − p₀ × RR)
Exact inverse. Requires baseline risk.
Delta method for CI (log scale)
SE(ln RR) = SE(ln OR) × |d ln RR / d ln OR| ∂RR/∂OR = (1−p₀) / (1−p₀+p₀×OR)² → use chain rule

OR / RR → RD (Risk Difference)

OR → RD
p₁ = p₀ × OR / (1 − p₀ + p₀ × OR) RD = p₁ − p₀
p₁ = treatment group event risk. RD = ARR (absolute risk reduction).
RR → RD
RD = p₀ × (RR − 1)
Delta method for CI (linear scale)
SE(RD) = SE(RR) × p₀ [from RR route] 95% CI: RD ± 1.96 × SE(RD)

RD / RR / OR → NNT

From RD (ARR)
NNT = 1 / |RD| = 1 / |p₀ − p₁|
When RD > 0 (harm), the measure is NNH (number needed to harm).
CI for NNT (reciprocal of CI for RD)
NNT_lower = 1 / |RD_upper| NNT_upper = 1 / |RD_lower|
CI bounds swap because of inversion. Confidence interval of NNT crossing 1/0 (infinity) indicates RD CI crossing zero (non-significant).

HR ⇄ OR ⚠ (Approximate)

Zhang & Yu 1998 (common events, rare events special case)
For rare events (p₀ < 10%): OR ≈ HR For common events, use: p₁ = 1 − (1−p₀)^HR OR = p₁(1−p₀) / (p₀(1−p₁))
Proportional hazards approximation. Requires baseline risk p₀ at a fixed time horizon. Always approximate — flagged.
Shor 2017 crude approximation (no p₀)
OR ≈ HR^1.42 [ln(OR) ≈ 1.42×ln(HR)]
⚠ Very crude. Use only when p₀ is unavailable. Error >20% for common events.

HR ⇄ RR ⚠ (Approximate)

Shor approximation
ln(RR) ≈ 0.7 × ln(HR) [i.e. RR ≈ HR^0.7]
⚠ Approximate only. Valid under proportional hazards and moderate event rates. Converts via RR route for RD, NNT.

OR ⇄ SMD (Standardised Mean Difference)

Chinn 2000 conversion
SMD = ln(OR) × √3 / π OR = exp(SMD × π / √3)
Assumes logistic distribution for binary outcome. π/√3 ≈ 1.8138. Reference: Chinn S. Stat Med 2000;19:3127–35.
Delta method (log scale)
SE(SMD) = SE(ln OR) × √3 / π

MD ⇄ SMD

Standard conversion
SMD = MD / SD_pooled MD = SMD × SD_pooled
SD_pooled = pooled standard deviation of the outcome. Must be supplied by user.
Delta method (linear scale)
SE(SMD) = SE(MD) / SD_pooled

CI Derivation (General)

SE from CI (log scale: OR, RR, HR)
SE(ln X) = [ln(upper) − ln(lower)] / (2 × 1.96)
SE from CI (linear scale: RD, MD, SMD)
SE(X) = (upper − lower) / (2 × 1.96)
95% CI from SE (log scale)
CI = [exp(ln X − 1.96×SE), exp(ln X + 1.96×SE)]
95% CI from SE (linear scale)
CI = [X − 1.96×SE, X + 1.96×SE]
ⓘ About MAConverter

What is MAConverter?

MAConverter is a browser-based universal effect size translator for meta-analysis and systematic reviews. It converts between OR, RR, RD, NNT, HR, SMD and MD with confidence intervals using the delta method.

Supported Conversions

  • OR ⇄ RR (exact, requires baseline risk p₀)
  • OR / RR → RD (exact, requires p₀)
  • RD / RR / OR → NNT (exact)
  • HR → OR / RR / RD / NNT (approximate, requires p₀)
  • OR ⇄ SMD (Chinn 2000)
  • MD ⇄ SMD (requires pooled SD)

CI Computation

All confidence intervals are propagated via the delta method. The standard error is extracted from the input CI on the appropriate scale (log for ratio measures, linear for difference measures), transformed, and back-transformed to produce 95% CI bounds.

Key References

  • Chinn S. A simple method for converting an odds ratio to effect size. Stat Med 2000;19:3127–35.
  • Zhang J, Yu KF. What's the relative risk? JAMA 1998;280:1690–1.
  • Shor E et al. A practical guide to harmonising data on relative treatment effects. Pharmacoepidemiol Drug Saf 2017.
  • Altman DG, Andersen PK. Calculating the number needed to treat. BMJ 1999;319:1309.

Limitations & Warnings

  • HR ↔ OR/RR conversions are approximate. They assume proportional hazards and a single fixed time horizon.
  • All results are labelled APPROX where formulas are approximate.
  • NNT is undefined when RD = 0 (no treatment effect).
  • OR ≈ RR only when baseline risk < ~10%.
